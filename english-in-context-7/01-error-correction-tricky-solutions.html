1. Despite of ‚Üí Despite the bad weather, we decided to go hiking.
"Despite" is never followed by "of". Use "in spite of" OR "despite" alone.
2. The teacher suggested us to study ‚Üí that we (should) study / studying harder.
"Suggest" is followed by gerund (-ing) or "that + subject + verb", NOT "object + infinitive".
3. She has been living in Vienna since ‚Üí for five years.
"For" + duration (5 years); "Since" + starting point (2020).
4. Neither the students nor the teacher were ‚Üí was aware.
With "neither...nor", the verb agrees with the NEAREST subject ("teacher" = singular).
5. If I would have known ‚Üí had known, I would have come earlier.
Third conditional: If + past perfect (NOT "would have" in the if-clause).
6. The information you gave me were ‚Üí was very helpful.
"Information" is uncountable (singular verb).
7. He denied to have taken ‚Üí having taken the money.
"Deny" is followed by gerund (-ing), NOT infinitive.
8. Hardly I had arrived ‚Üí Hardly had I arrived home when the phone rang.
Inversion after negative adverbs (hardly, scarcely, no sooner): auxiliary before subject.
9. My mother made me to clean ‚Üí clean my room.
"Make" + object + bare infinitive (without "to").
10. I could hardly keep my eyes opened ‚Üí open.
"Keep + object + adjective" (open, NOT past participle "opened").
11. According with ‚Üí According to the news report.
Fixed expression: "according TO" (NOT "with").
12. She is one of the most talented musicians who has ‚Üí have ever performed here.
"One of + plural noun + who + plural verb" (the relative clause refers to "musicians").
13. By the time you will arrive ‚Üí arrive, we will have already finished.
No future tense in time clauses (when, by the time, as soon as) ‚Äì use present simple.
14. I wish I would be ‚Üí were / was taller.
"I wish" about present: past simple (NOT "would"). "I wish I were" is more formal.
15. The police is ‚Üí are investigating.
"Police" is plural in British English (takes "are").
